[bold,center]Agility, Pattern Matching, and Problem Taste

[bold]Agility: stay light

[two-columns]
Ship before noon twice weekly. Build the smallest thing that can fail in front of reality.
One artificial constraint. Solve without your favorite tool.
Teach from a blank page weekly. No slides, no jargon.
Keep a kill list. Try to break your proudest approach every Friday.
Pattern matching: see the skeleton

Mechanism library, not paper library. One page per mechanism with diagram, where it appears, when it fails. Review five cards each morning.
Daily analogy drill, ten minutes. Force three analogies from unrelated domains. Note where the mapping breaks.
Redescribe four ways: sketch, user story, equation, code.
Interleave fields. Thirty minutes daily outside your discipline, extract one transferable principle.
Toy worlds. Keep three small sandboxes you can modify in an afternoon.
Problem taste: three filters

Consequence. Does it change a real decision or remove real cost? Log irritations with time or money cost.
Non-obviousness. Can a competent person solve it in a week with existing tools? If yes, drop.
Durability. Will the question matter in five years if tools change? If no, drop.
Pre-mortem. Write one page on how it fails in six months. If failures are about prestige, walk away.
[end-two-columns]

One notebook with three sections: irritations, mechanisms, analogies. Date everything.
Twelve favorite problems list. Update quarterly.
Personal ban list of overused tools or phrases.
Keep your loops faster than the culture around you.

[bold] Learning to See Differently

If I had to pick one, I would not pick novelty, or intelligence, or divergent thinking. I would pick seeing.

Creativity at its root is the capacity to perceive a hidden equivalence. You hold two frames at once that everyone else keeps separate, and you let them interfere until a third thing appears. Koestler called it bisociation. Poincaré described it as the sudden perception of a true relation that was already there. It feels like invention, but it is recognition.

That is why the habits we talked about work. Agility keeps your eyes close to the phenomenon so you have fresh material to see. Pattern matching builds a library of deep shapes, not surface features, so you have more frames to try. Problem taste filters for situations where a new equivalence would actually matter, because seeing is cheap, caring is rare.

Seeing is not passive. It has a structure:

Attention to friction. You notice where the world resists expectation. An irritation, a delay, a cost that should not exist. Most minds smooth it over. A creative mind lingers.
Holding the tension. You refuse the early closure of "this is like that other paper" or "this is just how it is done." You keep two unlike descriptions alive: the user story and the equation, the market stall and the packet queue, the enzyme and the search algorithm.
Testing the fit. You ask what the equivalence predicts. If traffic in Ho Chi Minh City really is a buffer with bursty arrivals, then metering lights should behave like TCP slow start. You ship the smallest test before noon. Seeing collapses into usefulness only when it makes a bet.
Novelty without a true equivalence is decoration. Intelligence without attention is recycling. The one thing you cannot outsource is noticing what is actually there, not what the literature trained you to expect.

Cultivate that, and creativity stops feeling mystical. You walk around with a quiet question: where is the same shape wearing a different costume today? Write one down daily. After a month, your mind begins to answer before you ask.
